(what)


As the phoenix rises from the ashes, so the vertical garden at the UmArts Campus. In this mythic journey of death and rebirth, Hanna Forsberg and Rana Zeeb have taken to the field, with support from Javier Avila Cariño and Teresa Frisan. They begin at the UmU department of Molecular Biology, collect lab supplies, then head to the Arts Campus with the intention of studying the evolving microbial diversity of the dying, soon to be reborn, vertical garden.


* This project is the bachelor thesis work of Hanna Forsberg and Rana Zeeb. Hanna Forsberg and Rana Zeeb develop most of the experimental set-up and carry on all the experimental work. Teresa Frisan and Javier Avilla Cariño perform mentoring support. The project is financed is Department of Molecular Biology.


(01)
(before take down)

At the Arts Campus, they sample the air, soil and leaves before the dying wall is removed. The intention is to characterise the microbiome of the dying ecosystem and surviving plants, then to later sample the reborn ecosystem. They sample:

/ Soil pockets with living plants
/ Soil pockets with dead plants
/ Leaves of living plants
/ Static water, in the drains

All sample locations are labelled with tape and a flag. Other student teams then harvest the still-living plants for rehabilitation and later replanting.


(02)
(after removal)

The vertical garden is then removed, and Hanna Forsberg and Rana Zeeb take air samples.

(03)
(laboratory)

In the lab, the samples are plated in different types of bacterial growth medium; the number of microbes are counted; each unique bacteria and fungus colony is characterised based on its morphology, capacity to grow in particular media, and motility capacity.


(04)
(objectives of the research)

/ Compare microbial composition between sectors with dead and living plants
/ Compare microbial composition of each sector hosting different types of still-living plants
/ Compare the amount and type of microorganisms dispersed in the air before and just after the removal of the dying vertical garden

(preliminary analysis)

The soil without plants has a higher microbial diversity than the soil with plants. This finding suggests that plants require a specific microbial ecosystem and possibly contribute to its determination.

Potential pathogenic bacteria has been found in the soil and air close to the vertical garden. The team is now mapping whether the presence of the plants restrict growth of these bacteria.

Samples collected from the water in close proximity to the vertical garden present a microbial pattern closely related to the air samples, rather than to the soil samples, suggesting cross-contamination between these environments.

As the new wall takes shape, Hanna Forsberg and Rana Zeeb will continue their quest, studying the microbial evolution of the new wall, which is being birthed from the ashes of the old one.

(expected contribution / sustainability and health )

/ Understand whether there is a correlation between plant survival and microbial microenvironment, which may be used to develop a soil with the microbial composition to support sustainable vertical gardens.
/ Assess the presence of potentially beneficial or pathogenic microorganisms in the air in proximity of a vertical garden, with the aim of promoting microbiologically safe urban cultivation approaches.


(to be continued)


The journey will continue following the microbial evolution in the new green wall born from the ashes of the old one.
